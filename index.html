Cybersecurity

Defends systems, networks, and programs from cyberattacks.

Cyberattacks

Usually try to steal sensitive data, extort money from users, or disrupt company activities.

These are the following tasks of cybersecurity professionals:

  • Using all channels to monitor technology and security threats.
  • Being aware through analyzing and evaluating threats.
  • Checking all systems for any potential issues.
  • Setting up security and a protocol.
  • Creating reports for shareholders.
  • Spreading awareness of security and its importance within the organization.

Four (4) Cybersecurity Career Path

Security Architect

Protects company computers and predicts computer hacker methods. They work at irregular times and follow security and attacking trends. IT specialists believe former hackers are the greatest security architects because they understand hackers.

Security Consultant

Advise and manage security practices to protect a company's assets. The expert evaluates security risks and vulnerabilities and creates action plans.

Ethical Hacker

Also known as White-hat hackers who are authorized to get into computer systems, networks, and apps to find vulnerabilities that bad hackers can attack.

Chief Information Security Officer (CISO)

Organizational data and information security are managed by the CISO. CISOs must identify their daily duties. Examples include data loss, program, access, and security activities.

Introduction to Risks, Threats, and Vulnerabilities

THE ANATOMY OF CYBERATTACK

Reconaissance

Hackers usually begin by collecting details about their target organization, such as network ranges, IP addresses, domain names, and key employee emails, especially those in IT. If they can't find these emails, they send phishing emails to other employees. They then scan for network vulnerabilities, which can take months. Once they gain access through these vulnerabilities or via compromised email accounts, they proceed to the next stage of their attack.

Attack

Once hackers gain access to the network, they aim to move freely within it. To do this, they need higher access privileges, so they use tools like rainbow tables to steal credentials and elevate their access to administrator levels. With these privileges, hackers can navigate the entire network undetected. They then steal sensitive information to sell online, encrypt data to demand a ransom, or sometimes alter or erase data for reasons other than financial gain.

Note: Rainbow tables are pre-made lists of password hashes used to crack passwords quickly. Instead of guessing each password one by one, hackers use these tables to find the matching password for a given hash faster.

Expansion

Hackers use malicious programs to infiltrate all systems on the network. These programs let attackers hide in multiple systems and regain access even after detection. This means hackers don't need higher access privileges to stay in the network.

Obfuscation

Hackers hide their tracks to mask the origins of the attack. They securely place their exploits in the system to avoid detection. The goal is to confuse forensic experts. To do this, they use techniques like spoofing, log cleaning, zombie accounts, and Trojan commands. Obfuscation is usually considered the final stage of a cyber attack.

THE CIA TRIAD

Confidentially

Ensures sensitive information is accessed only by authorized individuals and kept away from unauthorized ones. Implemented with security mechanisms like usernames, passwords, ACLs, and encryption. Information is categorized based on potential damage if leaked, and security measures are applied accordingly.

Integrity

Ensures information remains true and correct to its original state. Only authorized individuals can edit it, and it remains unchanged when at rest. Implemented with data encryption and hashing. Backups and redundant systems are crucial to maintain integrity against events like EMPs or server crashes.

Availability

Ensures information and resources are accessible to those who need them. Implemented through hardware maintenance, software patching, and network optimization. Redundancy, failover, RAID, and high-availability clusters mitigate the impact of hardware issues. Dedicated devices protect against downtime from attacks like DDoS.

Data Classification Standards

This standard aims to provide a structure for categorizing data based on its sensitivity, value, and importance. Data classification helps determine the essential security measures needed to protect it.

Understanding

The data management process starts with understanding what the data is, how it's classified, and where it's located. This cycle is continuous, as data may change and require reclassification over time.

Creating

Involves collecting data, conducting experiments, observing, and measuring or simulating scenarios.

Storing

Covers designing research, finding existing data, and capturing metadata to store data effectively.

Using

Encompasses data entry, digitization, validation, cleaning, anonymization when necessary, description, interpretation, and statistical analysis.

Sharing

Involves distributing and promoting data, controlling access, establishing copyright, producing research outputs, preparing data for preservation, and guiding users through the classification process.

Archiving

Includes migrating data to suitable formats and mediums, backing up and storing data, creating metadata and documentation, and managing archived data.

Destroying

Involves disposing of data, destroying paper records and electronic media, and securely erasing electronic data.

Security Concepts and Goals

Subjects and Objects of Security

People

Employees can create some of the greatest risks to cybersecurity. For example, cybercriminals might target employees with phishing e- mails to get them to click on a malicious link or divulge credentials.

Processes

This layer of cybersecurity ensures that IT teams have strategies in place to proactively prevent and to respond quickly and effectively in the event of a cybersecurity incident. A good incident response plan will provide an organization with repeatable procedures and an operational approach to address cybersecurity incidents to recover business processes as quickly and efficiently as possible.

Technology

It is important that IT teams do not implement isolated point solutions as they layer their defenses, but rather select those tools based on their ability to be integrated and automated to create a security fabric that can facilitate the rapid detection and mitigation of threats.

Emerging Technologies in Cybersecurity

1. Hardware authentication

Hardware authentication can be especially important when it comes to the Internet of Things (IoT) where the network of connected devices ensures that any device that seeks to be connected has the rights for connectivity to that particular network.

2. Cloud Technology

More business enterprises and government agencies have embraced cloud technology to store the vast amounts of information that they generate daily. Both private and public entities have doubled up their data center security by the use of Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS) services such as FireHost and Amazon.

3. Deep Learning

Deep learning, just like behavior analytics, focuses on anomalous behavior. Whenever artificial intelligence and machine learning systems are fed with the right data regarding potential systems security threats, they can make decisions on how to prevent hacks depending on their immediate environment without any human point.

Five (5) Types of Cybersecurity (Security rendered in the network)

Critical Infrastructure Security

This consists of the cyber-physical systems that modern societies rely on.

Examples: Electricity grid, water purification traffic lights, shopping centers, and hospitals

Application Security

This is more accessible over networks, causing the adoption of security measures during the development phase to be an imperative phase of the project.

Examples: Antivirus programs, firewalls, and encryption

Network Security

This ensures that internal networks are secured by protecting the infrastructure and inhibiting access to it.

Examples: Extra logins, new passwords, and application security (firewalls, monitored Internet access, antispyware, antivirus)

Cloud Security

It is a software-based security tool that protects and monitors data in cloud resources. Cloud providers are constantly creating and implementing new security tools to help enterprise users better secure their data.

Examples: Software as a Service, Infrastructure as a Service, private, on-site cloud, and virtualization

Internet of Things (IoT) Security

IoT refers to a wide variety of critical and non-critical cyber-physical systems like appliances, sensors, televisions, Wi-Fi routers, printers, and security cameras.

Examples: Connected security systems, thermostats, cars, electronic appliances, and speaker systems

Security Objectives

Security Policy

is a set of rules that applies to activities for the computer and communications resources that belong to an organization. The security policy defines what an organization wants to protect and what it expects of its system users.

Security Objectives

Resource Protection

The resource protection scheme ensures that only authorized users can access objects on the system. As such, carefully define the different categories of users that can access the system.

Authentication

The assurance or verification that the resource (human or machine) at the other end of the session really is what it claims to be. Solid authentication defends a system against the security risk of impersonation, in which a sender or receiver uses a false identity to access a system.

Authorization

This is an assurance that the person or computer at the other end of the session has permission to carry out the request. Authorization is the process of determining who or what can access the system resources or perform certain activities on a system.

Integrity

This is an assurance that the arriving information is the same as what was sent out. Understanding integrity requires understanding the concepts of data integrity and system integrity.

Data Integrity

Data is protected from unauthorized changes or tampering. Data integrity defends against the security risk of manipulation, or the act of intercepting and changing information to which s/he is not authorized.

System Integrity

The system provides consistent and expected results with expected performance.

Nonrepudiation

This is the proof that a transaction occurred, or that a user sent or received a message. Both the sender and the receiver agree that the exchange takes place.

Confidentiality

This is the assurance that sensitive information remains private and is not visible to an eavesdropper. Confidentiality is critical to total data security.

Auditing Security Activities

These monitor security-relevant events to provide a log of both successful and unsuccessful (denied) access.

IT Security Framework

1. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

It is a federal agency within the United States Department of Commerce. NIST’s mission is to develop and promote measurement, standards, and technology to enhance productivity, facilitate trade, and improve the quality of life.

The Cybersecurity Framework consists of three (3) main components:

  • Framework core – It describes expected cybersecurity procedures and results clearly.
  • Framework implementation tiers – provide context for cybersecurity risk management in businesses.
  • Framework profiles – These identify and prioritize cybersecurity improvements at an organization.

2. ISO/IEC – 27000 family

ISO 27000 is a systematic approach to managing sensitive information securely (also known as the Information Security Management System [ISMS]). It includes managing risk for people, processes, and IT systems.

ISO 27000 includes a six-part approach:

  • Define a security policy.
  • Define the scope of the ISMS.
  • Conduct a risk assessment.
  • Manage identified risks.
  • Select control objectives and controls to be implemented.
  • Prepare a statement of applicability.

3. Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)

It was initiated to ensure businesses process card payments and to help reduce card fraud.

This payment standard has principle requirements, all of which are covered by these six (6) categories:

  • Build and maintain a secure network.
  • Protect card data.
  • Maintain a vulnerability program.
  • Implement strong access control measures.
  • Regularly monitor and test networks.
  • Maintain an Information security policy

Security Architecture

Security architectures

Unified designs that address a particular scenario's needs and risks. The timing and location of security controls are also specified while the design is usually repeatable.

Security architecture includes structure and connections as well as the design concepts and security control specifications in distinct papers. ocation of security controls are also specified while the design is usually repeatable.

The key attributes of security architecture are as follows:

  • Benefits – Standardization makes security architecture affordable. Reusing controls reduces design costs.
  • Form – Security architecture is similar to IT architecture but has several forms. Relationship graphs, principles, and typical controls are included.
  • Drivers – Security controls are determined based on these four (4) factors: (Risk Management, Benchmarking and good practice, Financial, Legal and regulatory)

The key phases in the security architecture process are as follows:

  • Architecture Risk Assessment – evaluates essential assets, vulnerabilities, and security threats' business impact.
  • Security Architecture and Design – Security service architecture supports corporate risk exposure targets.
  • Implementation – It manages secure services and processes. Assurance services ensure runtime implementation matches security policy, standards, architecture, and risk management.
  • Operations and Monitoring – Daily processes include threat, vulnerability, and threat management. Besides system security, the operational state is managed.
